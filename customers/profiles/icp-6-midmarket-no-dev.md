## Key Pain Points

- **Clicks are declining and leadership is losing faith in SEO.** AI Overviews, zero-click results, and SERP volatility are visibly impacting traffic. The team knows why but struggles to explain it convincingly — and lacks the data to show what to do instead.
- **They have no developer support, despite having a larger site.** A mid-market company might have 50,000+ pages, multiple content types, and complex site architecture — but the SEO team still can't get engineering time for automations or technical work.
- **Scale makes manual processes unsustainable.** An audit that works fine for 1,000 pages becomes impossible at 50,000. Without automation, they're always behind.
- **Reporting to leadership is a credibility problem.** When ranking and traffic metrics are declining, the team needs a new narrative — but they don't have the data infrastructure to build one. AI visibility metrics would help, but they have no way to measure them.
- **Fragmented tooling creates data silos.** Different team members use different tools. There's no unified view of the site, competitors, or performance.
- **Without pre-built starting points, they don't know where to begin with AI-era SEO.** This team has the intent to adapt but will revert to "just give me more reports" behavior if the platform doesn't show them what good looks like.

---

## Core Value Proposition

The platform gives this team both the automation infrastructure and the opinionated starting points they need to adapt. Pre-built workflows for AI visibility monitoring, content auditing, and reporting mean they're not starting from scratch. The unified data layer connects GSC, GA4, keyword data, crawl data, and competitor signals — giving leadership a single, coherent story about performance. The workflow builder replaces the developer dependency that's been blocking them. And AI visibility monitoring gives the team a new, forward-looking metric to present to stakeholders — shifting the narrative from "our rankings dropped" to "here's where we're winning in AI search and why."

**In short:** a new internal narrative backed by real data, and the automation to finally match site scale.

---

## Assumptions About Their Priorities

- **Internal credibility is the #1 priority.** The team needs leadership to believe SEO is still strategic. This means data, not opinions — and especially forward-looking metrics around AI search.
- **They need opinionated starting points.** Left to explore an open-ended platform, they'll be overwhelmed. Pre-built workflows for the most common mid-market use cases (site audits, content performance, competitor monitoring, AI visibility) are essential to adoption.
- **Budget is available but requires a clear ROI story.** They can justify spend if they can show what it replaces or what it enables. Time-to-insight and automation value are the right levers.
- **They're evaluating the platform partly on whether it makes SEO easier to defend internally.** If it helps them present a better story to leadership, that's worth paying for.
- **Scale is a qualification criterion.** If the platform can't handle a large site without degrading, they'll reject it quickly.

---

## Ideal Roles

- **SEO Director / Head of SEO** — Owns the function; needs to defend SEO internally with better data and narratives.
- **Senior SEO Manager** — Manages day-to-day execution; evaluates on workflow usability and automation depth.
- **SEO Team Lead** — Coordinates across team members; values consistency and shared tooling.
- **VP of Marketing / CMO** — Executive sponsor; cares about credibility of SEO as a channel and forward-looking metrics.
- **Digital Marketing Manager** — May oversee SEO alongside other channels; interested in cross-channel visibility.
